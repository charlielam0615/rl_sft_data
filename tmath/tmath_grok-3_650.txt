[IMP]  
To count intersecting pairs, consider choosing 4 distinct vertices from the 7 vertices of the heptagon, since each pair of diagonals is defined by four endpoints. The number of ways to choose 4 vertices is:  
\[ \binom{7}{4} = \frac{7 \cdot 6 \cdot 5 \cdot 4}{4 \cdot 3 \cdot 2 \cdot 1} = 35 \]  

For four vertices in cyclic order (e.g., \( a, b, c, d \)), label them as \( 1, 2, 3, 4 \) in clockwise order after sorting cyclically. The possible diagonals are \( 1-3 \) and \( 2-4 \), \( 1-4 \) and \( 2-3 \), or \( 1-2 \) and \( 3-4 \), but we need diagonals that are valid (non-adjacent vertices) and intersect inside. In a convex quadrilateral formed by four points, exactly one of the three pairings results in diagonals that cross inside. For example, in quadrilateral \( 1, 2, 3, 4 \):  
- Diagonals \( 1-3 \) and \( 2-4 \): These intersect inside the quadrilateral (and thus inside the heptagon).  
- Diagonals \( 1-4 \) and \( 2-3 \): These do not intersect inside (they are sides or intersect outside).  
- Diagonals \( 1-2 \) and \( 3-4 \): These are sides, not diagonals.  

Thus, for each set of 4 vertices, there is exactly 1 way to choose two diagonals that intersect inside the heptagon. Therefore, the number of intersecting diagonal pairs is equal to the number of ways to choose 4 vertices:  
\[ \binom{7}{4} = 35 \]  
[/IMP]
